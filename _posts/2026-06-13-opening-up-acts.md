Like many people, writing things down helps me understand them better. The action of having a paper book, paper notebook, and my preferred writing implement lets me retain and understand what I'm reading in a way that I don't connect with in a digitial format. Part of that probably comes from lifestyle. I spend my professional and personal time online, the things that demand my attention are so prevalent that the best way to step away from that is to use a different medium.

...The problem with that is that paper notebooks are not exactly the most searchable format, what is good for ingestion is not what is good for review. Naturally the answer to that is an online blog.

I've started reading in the book of Acts. Most of my knowledge of scripture comes from the KJV translation. My ability to quote scripture or talk about topics comes from that basis. There is nothing inherently wrong with that translation, but trying to understand words or phrases because of the translation rather than trying to understand the meaning of what has been written can result in more time spent at the surface and less time diving into the meaning itself. 

I typically turn to the NLT translation now as a starting point, and then compare with other versions to get a fuller sense. My reading of Acts showcased those differences quite well this time. For instance, let's look at Acts 1:4 across a few translations:

| Translation | Text |
|-------------|------|
| [KJV - King James Version](https://www.blueletterbible.org/kjv/act/1/1-26/s_1019001) | And, being **assembled** together with \[them\]... |
| [NLT - New Living Translation](https://www.blueletterbible.org/nlt/act/1/1-26/s_1019001) | Once when he was **eating** with them... |
| [ESV - English Standard Version](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/act/1/1-26/s_1019001) | And while **staying** with them... |
| [NET - New English Translation](https://www.blueletterbible.org/net/act/1/1-26/s_1019001) | While he was with them... |

Quite the difference between these!
Let's dive deeper into what's going on here, for simpllicity I'll stick with NLT to see the larger portion.

> 3 During the forty days after he suffered and died, he appeared to the apostles from time to time, and he proved to them in many ways that he was actually alive. And he talked to them about the Kingdom of God. 4 Once when he was eating with them, he commanded them, "Do not leave Jerusalem until the Father sends you the gift he promised, as I told you before. 5 John baptized with water, but in just a few days you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit." 6 So when the apostles were with Jesus, they kept asking him, "Lord, has the time come for you to free Israel and restore our kingdom?" 7 He replied, "The Father alone has the authority to set those dates and times, and they are not for you to know. 8 But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes upon you. And you will be my witnesses, telling people about me everywhere--in Jerusalem, throughout Judea, in Samaria, and to the ends of the earth." 9 After saying this, he was taken up into a cloud while they were watching, and they could no longer see him. 10 As they strained to see him rising into heaven, two white-robed men suddenly stood among them. 11 "Men of Galilee," they said, "why are you standing here staring into heaven? Jesus has been taken from you into heaven, but someday he will return from heaven in the same way you saw him go!" 12 Then the apostles returned to Jerusalem from the Mount of Olives, a distance of half a mile.
> - *Acts 1:3-12 NLT*

There are a couple different pieces here:
- During the forty days
  - Timeline Luke is writing about, a 40 day period
- Acts 1:4 | And, Once, While
  - This verse and conversation happens *somewhere* in the 40 days
- Acts 1:6 | So when the apostles were with Jesus
  - The only separator in NLT between the conversation in Acts 1:4 and Acts 1:6 is "So"
- Acts 1:9 | After saying this
  - This is our direct link, after Acts 1:6-8 comes Acts 1:9, closely tied
- Acts 1:12 | Returned to Jerusalem from the Mount of Olives
  - We have a starting and ending location

On a casual reading it's easy to look at this as two kind of broad statements by Luke, the first being that he is talking about a 40 day period, and the second being that the conversation that follows happens during this walk from Jerusalem to the Mount of Olives. And I don't think there is anything particularly wrong with that reading. Jesus tells the disciples that they need to wait for what will be sent before beginning the Great Commission, and the disciples, digesting this information that Jesus is about to leave and send the Comforter previously promised, ask a fairly reasonable question if this is the end times and Israel will have the promised victory. Jesus responds saying they (and us) are not permitted to know the time, but are granted power and authority in the time inbetween, from Jesus returning to Heaven until he comes back again, we are empowered to act under His authority.

If we look at this from the NLT, or ESV, the word choice helps introduce a more explicit spacing of events. Jesus and the disciples didn't stop at McDonalds on the way to the Mount of Olives. So whether eating or staying, there is a time that is longer and more casual than just a walk out from Jerusalem. But with this information, it changes the scale of things. Now the disciples are ingesting what Jesus said, trying to understand, and then asking a question for clarification. Gone are the days where the disciples are firing off the first thing in their head, and instead are not only looking at what Jesus said but trying to understand the timeline through the scriptures that have been written so far. They know the end times is coming, they know what it means for Israel, and they are studiously comparing this to what Jesus is saying to understand the times and seasons.

This distinction has made me wonder about the actions that take place during a waiting period. What do I do when I'm waiting? Am I sitting static, being a little statue collecting duest, or am I executing the typical actions of living? Am I eating? Sleeping? Are the standard functions happening even in a waiting period? Yes, obviously, outside of very specific exceptions (like preparing for a surgery), if there is anything I'm physically waiting for, the physical actions of life continue. I still eat, sleep, and do the things that keep myself alive.

So what happens in a spiritual waiting period? The thing I struggle with is that if I'm waiting to hear from God I feel like I'm in stasis. Like if I do something it's going to be wrong and cause problems. But the basic functions of spiritual life still need to happen. Assembling together with belivers, check. Eating, consuming the word and gaining strength, check. Praying not just for the answer but also the normal prayer life, check. 

But saying that is a lot easier than doing it! There is a real but sometimes overreactionary fear that doing *something* will result in going outside God's plans. Sarah moved things along after looking at a problem and not seeing a human solution. Zecheriah confronted God's messenger and demanded proof because of the same nonexistent human solution. Elijah burnt out in between messages from God. There are examples throughout scripture of someone waiting and bad things happening. It's a risky thing to look at those examples and take away the idea that nothing can be done at all.

It's been exciting to start diving into Acts again seeing it through the fresh lens of a new translation. I'm excited to see what I come across next.
